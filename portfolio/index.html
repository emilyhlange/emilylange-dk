Project 01 — Visma e-conomic, 2022–Present
Designing how a design system collaborates
Design Systems Governance Stakeholder Management Workshop Facilitation
Context

As the organisation scaled, the design system struggled to keep pace. More teams were shipping. Delivery pressure was increasing. But the system lacked the structures needed to support this growth — no shared intake model, no clear ownership boundaries, no consistent understanding of what the system should be responsible for.

The team operated reactively. Work arrived through conversations. Demand was invisible. Local decisions accumulated into long-term system debt. The design system risked becoming a service function, rather than infrastructure.

My role

Product Designer, Design Systems. I identified the structural gap and led the initiative — from research through to implementation and adoption.

Approach

I shifted focus toward designing how the system collaborates — not just what it delivers. The gap wasn't only structural in the UI. It was structural in how teams engaged with the system.

  • Introduced contribution thinking — moving from requests to shared ownership
  • Structured feedback as a formal input to decision-making
  • Built a formal intake and prioritisation approach
  • Defined clearer boundaries between system work and product work
Outcome
  • Incoming needs became visible and prioritisable
  • Stakeholder loops were closed — teams knew the status of their requests
  • The design system repositioned: from a place to request UI, to a partner that guides decisions
Reflection
"A design system is more than a component team. It needs to support, advocate, and collaborate. When product pressure builds, teams don't just need UI — they need guidance. Design systems scale through trust and shared ownership."
Project 02 — Visma e-conomic, 2022–Present
Systematising product language
Content Design UX Writing Design Systems Cross-team Alignment
Context

While design matured, language remained unstructured. No content designers. No shared writing ownership. Designers wrote UI copy independently — and as the product grew more international, inconsistencies became increasingly visible.

No one was to blame — there were no shared expectations. But if a product can't demonstrate care in how it communicates, why should users trust that it works?

My role

Self-initiated. I identified the gap, built the case internally, and led the effort — while simultaneously upskilling myself in content design. As the daughter of a technical writer, raised in a Danish/English household, I had a sharp eye for where the product's language was failing.

Approach

I treated language as part of the system — not a layer on top of design, but a direct contributor to experience quality.

  • Built a structured writing library with reusable microcopy patterns
  • Aligned terminology across departments
  • Integrated writing guidance directly into designers' workflows
  • Avoided a static guide — embedded shared language into everyday practice instead
Outcome

Writing became a shared discipline. Designers started aligning terminology unprompted, requesting new patterns, and auditing copy in their own areas. A brand-writing group formed organically.

  • Reduced cognitive effort through consistent terminology
  • Fewer writing inconsistencies across flows
  • A culture shift: writing went from afterthought to habit
Reflection
"I took it upon myself to close a gap no one else was watching. That's the work I find most meaningful — identifying what's quietly breaking, and building the structure to fix it."
Project 03 — Visma e-conomic, 2022–Present
Building stability in a high-speed environment
Foundations Tokens Documentation Design Systems
Context

Over three years, the design team more than doubled in size. Adoption was already high — but without foundational guidance, usage became fragmented. Patterns diverged. Layout logic varied. Delivery capacity increased, but consistency did not.

The challenge

Foundational work is difficult to prioritise when delivery pressure is high. We had to reframe what progress looked like: working slowly wasn't delay — it was preparation to move faster later.

Approach

We built stability through foundations — starting from scratch where needed, without redesigning everything at once.

  • Built an entirely new token system, starting from atoms up
  • Simplified the colour scale using a systematic approach
  • Established clearer radius and border logic
  • Refined typography for flexibility and consistency

Documentation became central — not to describe redesigns, but to guide better usage of what already existed.

Outcome
  • Greater consistency across teams
  • Reduced layout divergence across the product
  • Faster onboarding for new designers
  • A stronger baseline for future component work
Reflection
"The goal wasn't uniformity. It was predictability — enabling teams to move faster without fragmenting the experience."
