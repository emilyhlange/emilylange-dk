Master Thesis: Designing for low self-esteem [2021]

Experiences of low self-esteem and low self-worth are often associated with a growing tendency of needing to perform more and better in social contexts, in school and academia, at work and on social media. To delve further into this problem, we began to ask ourselves questions that would either refute or confirm our assumptions on this issue. These questions included, What affects young adult’s self-confidence and self-esteem? What are methods for improving self-esteem? What digital tools exist in helping young adults address these feelings, and do they actually work? We initially found that social media usage and social pressures to overperform in various areas might be part of the issue. As aspiring digital designers, we naturally found an interest and a certain feeling of responsibility to investigate whether specific design features could be a causing factor in experiencing low self-esteem. Further, we wanted to explore how a digital artifact could be designed with more ethichhal responsibility towards the user’s intended and unintended emotional experience. In this thesis project, we are working with the following research question.

How can we assist young women in practicing self-appreciation through a digital artifact, and how can this improve their self-esteem and happiness long-term?

Summary: In attempting to answer our research question, we explore the qualities and dynamics of self-esteem in young women, with the intention of creating a design artifact that can assist in practicing self-appreciation, as a means of addressing feelings of low self-esteem. We did this by applying methods grounded in design research. Our primary method for gathering empirical data was development and use of a cultural probe deployed with five young women based in Denmark for four weeks. This was followed by a participatory workshop to evaluate the experiences with the designed features in the cultural probe. Finally, this has led to a collection of design arguments grounded in our empirical data. These design arguments are supported by a design workbook, elaborating on possible applications of our design arguments.

In collaboration with Anne Katrine Svejgaard Barrett.
